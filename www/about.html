About

Introduction

My name is Brice, and I am a data scientist and machine learning/AI programmer.

My data science interests are primarily in developing improvements to the data pipeline. My background in addictions treatment, has given me a strong understanding of the importance of having outcome measures that are rooted in theory and accurately depict the relationships involved. My tools of choice are Python, NumPy, Pandas, Sci-kit Learn, Tensorflow and Caffe but I have experience with R, Torch, and Keras.

My goal as a data scientist is to be able to provide the public with data on social issues, to inform social service programs through careful data collection and analysis, and to develop data driven intitiatives for social good.

About Me

I am a Washington native and have spent most of my life in the Pacific Northwest. I live in Lakewood with my son, Alexander and our golden retriever Lightning. My hobbies include cooking, hiking, music production, R/C cars, video games, and golfing.

My Career in Addictions

My interest in pursuing data science primarily grew from a career working in addictions and a desire to build better interventions in the treatment of clinical and public health addictions problems. There are major discrepancies between the established addictions literature and the actual implementation of therapeutic interventions in the Northwest. As I grew in my career and began to explore the field of addictions and mental health, I found myself enagaged in something that was more of an art than a science. I chose to pursue a career in addicitons after my own battle with substance abuse, as I have been clean and sober since 03/14/2009, and have been an active member of the recovery community since. During my navigation of the (public) mental health field, I encountered systemic forces that prevented the true application of addictions technology on the ground. I continue to advocate for the involvement of people with lived experience in all areas in health systems organization and for the implementation of evidence based practices that move away from the over-reliance on the medical model in the psychiatric sciences. I am able to voice my support for those battling addictions through my position on the board of directors for Dual Diagnosis Anonymous and have been actively involved in the peer movement as both an advocate and as a provider. To learn more about the peer movement and efforts to bring evidence based practices and peer support services to mental health treatment, please visit Mad in America. If you would like to support reformation efforts in mental health care, please visit the Foundation for Excellence in Mental Health, and If you'd like to get involved in efforts to bring a safe consumption space to Washington State, please check out Vocal-WA.
